Bring my father home


BBC link

On December 11th 1969, Mr. Hwang’s father, Hwang Won, boarded the Korean Air YS-11 flight to go on a business trip. Within 10 minutes, his plane was hijacked by a North Korean agent. While 39 passengers were returned, 11 abductees, including Mr. Hwang’s father, were not. \n \n Team Hwang is a group of volunteers who have come together to join Mr Hwang in raising international awareness of this case and help bring his father back home to South Korea. \n \n Crimes of forced abduction committed by North Korea are vast in number and ongoing. By raising our voices, we will help pave the way for resolving hundreds of other abduction cases committed by North Korea.

Goals


By joining Hwang In-cheol in his efforts to bring his father home, we are standing up against impunity and North Korea’s crimes against humanity.



Find out about the fate and whereabouts of Hwang Won


North Korea continues to deny Hwang In-cheol access to any information on his father’s current whereabouts. Despite having made numerous requests (e.g., inter-Korean family reunions, letters to ICRC and UN) for confirmation of his father’s vital status, Pyongyang continually refuses to verify whether he is alive or not.


Team Hwang aims to mobilize grassroots movement in support of Hwang In-cheol so that North Korea has no choice but to disclose correct information about his father’s situation.



Secure Hwang Won’s right to return to South Korea


Everyone has the right to freely stay or leave his or her country, and to be with one’s family. That right has been blatantly violated for Hwang Won and his family for the last 50 years.


Team Hwang aims to uphold these basic rights and reunite a father with his family.



Raise awareness, both domestically and internationally, of the 1969 hijacking of Korean Air YS-11 flight


This is the only plane hijacking case in history that remains unresolved, which is largely due to lack of awareness and interest. Team Hwang recalls similar cases of North Korean abductions that were successfully resolved as a result of widespread awareness and a willingness to take action.

(See ‘Other abductions’ page)

Team Hwang aims to accumulate worldwide pressure on North Korea to investigate this crime and provide relief to Hwang Won and the other hijacking victims.



Raise awareness, both domestically and internationally, of the forced abductions committed by North Korea


North Korea’s abductions remain an ongoing issue that targets many countries, including in Asia, the Middle East, North America and Europe.


Team Hwang hopes that by raising awareness of Hwang Won’s case, we will pave the way to solving hundreds of other abduction cases.



Ensure accountability


Since 1983, North Korea has been a state party to the Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Seizure of Aircraft, under which anyone who hijacks a civilian aircraft should be extradited or prosecuted “without exception whatsoever”. Pyongyang continues to claim that the KAL flight was voluntarily diverted by the pilots and that the passengers who currently remain in North Korea do so by their own will. Neither of these claims have been verified.


Team Hwang believes in upholding justice and humanitarian principles that have been agreed upon by the international community, including North Korea.

On December 11th 1969, 4 crew members and 47 passengers boarded the Korean Air Lines YS-11 aircraft in Gwangwon-do. Just 10 minutes after take off, one of the passengers rose from his seat and entered the cockpit. This passenger was a North Korean sleeper agent called Cho Ch'ang-hǔi (조창희). Suddenly, the plane changed direction and was joined by 3 Korean People’s Air Force fighter jets. The plane landed in Sǒndǒk Airfield, North Korea, and North Korean soldiers then proceeded to board the aircraft, blindfold the passengers and instruct them to disembark. Every day of captivity, the passengers were subjected to 4 hours of attempted indoctrination. The night after the hijacking, 100,000 people in South Korea took to the streets in a mass rally to protest the incident, burning an effigy of Kim Il-Sung. On 25th December, North Korea finally responded and proposed to hold talks on the matter. By the following January, the North had pledged to repatriate all 50 hostages. On the 14th February 1970, after 66 days of captivity, the hostages were to be returned to South Korea at the Joint Security Area at Panmunjom. However, on the day of repatriation, only 39 passengers stepped foot on South Korean soil. The 4 crew members and 7 other passengers were missing. According to the North Korean government, these 11 hostages had voluntarily chosen to remain in North Korea. Until this day, that statement remains unverified. An explanation for the hijacking remains unclear. North Korea stated that the pilots themselves had redirected the aircraft in protest against the current policies of the president of South Korea, Park Chung-hee, however passengers have refuted these claims. One passenger stated that they looked out the window of the aircraft despite instructions from the North Korean guards, and saw the hijacker being driven away in a black sedan. As for the whereabouts of the 11 remaining hostages, North Korea continues to withhold this information from the families. At the Families’ Committee 2008 Inaugural meeting, a veteran from the National Intelligence Service, Song Yeong-in, commented that they were most likely detained in North Korea for their propagandic value and skilled expertise. Oh Kil-nam, who had briefly defected to North Korea in 1986, claimed that he had met the two flight attendants and the two Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation employees, Hwang Won and Gim Bongju. He reported that they were employed to issue propaganda broadcasts to the South. Oh was also told by his daughter that the captain of the aircraft and the first officer (?) were employed at the Korean People’s Air Force. In 2001, the mother of the flight attendant, Seong Gyeong-hui, was permitted to enter North Korea and see her daughter as one of the family reunions agreed to in the June 15th North–South Joint Declaration. In North Korea, Seong and the other flight attendant, Jeong Gyeong-suk, remained friends and were living in the same town.

Hwang In-cheol was just two years-old when his father, Hwang Won, boarded the flight for a business trip. As a broadcaster at Munhwa Broadcasting Corporation (MBC), Hwang Won was appointed on behalf of a former broadcast director, Baek Gi-Hong, to go to the United States. After the two year-old tried to block his father’s way out the door, he anxiously awaited his father’s return home. Decades later, Hwang In-cheol is still waiting. But/However, when Christmas came and his father still hadn’t come home, Hwang In-Cheol sadly concluded that his father no longer loved them and decided to leave home for good. Only at the age of 9 did Hwang In-cheol’s uncle come to learn the terrible truth/reality of what had happened to his father. The reasons for Hwang Won’s continued detention was revealed by testimonies from the 39 KAL passengers who managed to return. They stated that during indoctrination classes, Hwang Won had refused to accept any of the communist beliefs that were enforced upon him. Angering the North Korean soldiers, he was dragged away and separated from the rest of the hostages. When the other hostages saw him again on February 7th, 1970, Hwang Won was singing, “Ga-go-pa”, which means “I want to go back home”, and once again, the soldiers dragged him away. This was the last time that he was seen by any of the 39 passengers. Over the years, Hwang In-cheol’s hope to be reunited with his father was slowly eroding. North Korea’s continual denial of his father left Hwang In-cheol….. However, in 2000, the two Koreas had agreed to include South Korean abductees at reunions for families separated by the war. A step in the right direction. His hopes were once again fuelled when in 2001, Seong Gyeong-hui, another KAL hostage, was permitted to meet with her mother. But 5 years later, when the North Korean Red Cross stated that they were "unable to confirm" his father’s whereabouts, this hope turned into bold determination; Hwang In-cheol refused to accept to stand down against North Korea’s outright injustices. The last Hwang In-cheol heard of his father’s whereabouts was from a renowned broker (should we explain what this means?), codenamed ‘Superman’. He told Hwang In-cheol that he knew of his father’s whereabouts and could try to bring him to South Korea. In order to verify these claims, Hwang In-cheol gave Superman a set of questions which only his father would know the answer to. Through complex communication lines, Superman was able to contact Hwang Won and provide the right answers. Once verification was made, Hwang In-cheol and the broker sought confirmation from Hwang Won that he wanted to return to South Korea and then proceeded to plan his escape. In February 2013, Hwang Won made it as far as Sinujiu city and from here, he only needed to get on a boat. However, Kim Jong-un’s nuclear testing around this time meant that security was heightened and borders were being even more heavily guarded. Hwang Won lost the fight to return home and was recaptured by North Korean soldiers.
